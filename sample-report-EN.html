Reading the elements

Wood 38% — Peers. At 38%, Wood is the chart's largest force, but it belongs to the Peers category—Allies and Rivals—rather than to the Day Master's own root alone. Earth 25% — Wealth. Earth registers at 25% through two Steward placements in the Hour Stem and Year Stem, plus hidden Earth within multiple branches. As the Wealth element for Yi Wood, Earth represents resources, material accumulation, and practical responsibility. Its weight is considerable for a chart where the self is not fully strong, meaning the wealth demands of life can press heavily on him before he has built sufficient inner reserves.

Metal 25% — Officer. Metal also sits at 25%, concentrated dramatically in the Month Pillar—both stem (Geng) and branch (Shen) are Metal, making the Governor archetype doubly present in the most powerful natal position. Metal cuts and shapes Wood; for Yi Wood, the Governor represents discipline, authority, structure, and external pressure. This concentration is the defining structural tension of the chart: the force most present is also the one that bears down hardest on the self.

Fire 12% — Output. Fire occupies only 12% of the chart, appearing mainly in the Year Branch Wu (午) and its hidden stems. As the Output element—the Creator and Maverick archetypes—it represents this man's expressive, communicative, and creative energies. Its thinness means that sustained self-expression or creative output can feel effortful unless consciously cultivated; brilliance exists but must be deliberately fanned.

Water 0% — Resource. Water is entirely absent at 0%—there is not a single Water stem or branch in the four pillars. Yet Water is the primary Useful Element for this chart, the resource that nourishes Yi Wood and, critically, bridges and softens the Metal-into-Wood pressure cycle. This natal void means the most needed element must come entirely from the luck and annual cycle, making those Water-bearing decades profoundly important to this man's trajectory.

Metal presses Wood with no Water to mediateThe chart's central structural problem is precise: a double-Metal Governor in the Month Pillar bears down on a Yin Wood self that has no natal Water to act as the bridge between Metal and Wood. In classical five-element flow, Metal generates Water and Water feeds Wood—but when Water is absent, Metal cuts directly and without softening. This creates a man who lives under sustained external pressure—from institutions, authority, duty, and expectation—without the natural replenishment that Water resources would supply.
