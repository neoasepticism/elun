IThe Chart

The chart belongs to a Yang Metal (庚) Day Master born in the coldest month of the Ox — deep winter, when Metal is seasonally crisp and consolidated, receiving Earth nourishment from the month branch and Rival reinforcement from both year and month stems. The engine marks him Near-Balanced and mildly strong, with Metal commanding half the elemental weight of the chart. Three Rivals crowd the stems; the Challenger appears once in the year branch and once hidden in the day branch; the Governor surfaces in the day branch and its hidden stems. IIIAppearance & Presence 形象

Yang Metal (庚), strong and near-balanced, projects the classical image of well-tempered steel: strong-boned, angular, the features sharply and cleanly cut. The dominant elements are Metal and Fire — Metal suggests definition and structural precision, Fire adds luminosity and animated expression, a complexion with warmth and visible radiance. The Scholar structure (Earth feeding Metal) adds a grounded, substantial quality without tipping into the soft roundedness of a pure Earth chart. The composite image is a large-framed, strikingly handsome man with a bright, open face, strong jaw, and an almost geometric symmetry — powerful yet somehow elegant. Against the public record, this mapping is unusually clean. Muhammad Ali was documented from youth as extraordinarily good-looking — 'a beautiful, unmarked face' was the phrase used after his Olympic gold — with a physique that combined power and athleticism rather than the heavy-set thickness of many heavyweights. The Fire in both the day and year branches does appear to manifest in that noted luminosity: Ali's face was described repeatedly as radiant and expressive, his complexion warm and clear. The Metal angularity maps to the strong jaw and the precise, almost chiseled structure of his features. The one partial miss: classical 庚 Metal strong suggests a larger, more imposing frame, and while Ali was undeniably a large man, his movement was famously light and fluid — qualities more Water or Wood than Metal. The chart's single strongest physical implication is the face: Fire-illuminated Metal yields features that are simultaneously sharp-cut and radiantly alive, and the public record confirms that precisely.

VThe Signature

Every decade in this chart stages the same collision: a blade of Yang Metal, already crowded with peers and well-fed by resources, searching for a channel — Water, Output, expression — that the chart structurally withholds. When external channels opened (the ring, the platform, the crowd), the force flowed and the record was made. When channels were closed — by law, by illness, by age — the force turned inward, and the damage accrued. The Scholar Structure implies a man who needs institutional legitimacy to operate at full power, and the repeated story of his life is institutions simultaneously conferring and revoking that legitimacy, with Ali's own surplus force filling the gap in between. The chart does not explain Muhammad Ali's greatness. It explains, with some precision, the particular shape of his struggle: a man with more force than the world consistently knew how to accommodate, and more principle than the institutions around him were prepared to honor.

VIIThe Record

1942 · 0Born Cassius Marcellus Clay Jr. in segregated Louisville, Kentucky, to a sign-painter father and a domestic-worker mother; a strikingly good-looking, physically gifted, verbally quick child.
1954 · 12After his bicycle was stolen, a policeman who ran a boxing gym told the furious twelve-year-old he had better learn to fight first; he began boxing that year.
1960 · 18Won the light-heavyweight gold medal at the Rome Olympics; turned professional the same year with dazzling speed and a beautiful, unmarked face.
1964 · 22At twenty-two, shocked the world by defeating the fearsome Sonny Liston for the heavyweight title; announced his conversion to the Nation of Islam and took the name Muhammad Ali.
1967 · 25Refused induction into the US Army on religious and political grounds — 'I ain't got no quarrel with them Viet Cong' — was stripped of his title, banned from boxing, and convicted of draft evasion. Lost his prime fighting years.
1970 · 28The conviction was overturned by the Supreme Court in 1971; he returned to the ring after three and a half lost years.
1971 · 29Lost the 'Fight of the Century' to Joe Frazier — his first professional defeat, in a brutal fifteen-round war.
1974 · 32Regained the heavyweight title by knocking out the seemingly invincible George Foreman in Zaire — the 'Rumble in the Jungle', using the improvised rope-a-dope. One of the great comebacks in sport.
1975 · 33Beat Frazier in the 'Thrilla in Manila', a near-death third fight both men called the closest thing to dying they had known.
1978 · 36Lost the title to Leon Spinks, then regained it seven months later — becoming the first man to win the heavyweight championship three times.
1981 · 39Retired after two final losses, his speed visibly gone and his speech beginning to slur.
1984 · 42Diagnosed with Parkinson's syndrome, widely linked to the accumulated blows of his long career; his voice and movement slowly diminished over the following decades.
1996 · 54Lit the Olympic cauldron at the Atlanta Games with visibly trembling hands — a silent, luminous global moment that redefined his public image from provocateur to beloved elder.
2016 · 74Died at seventy-four after decades with Parkinson's, mourned worldwide as 'The Greatest' — as much for the title he sacrificed on principle as for the ones he won.
