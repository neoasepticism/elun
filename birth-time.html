Don't know what time you were born?

You're far from alone — and it stops far fewer readings than people think. Here's how to find your birth time, and what your chart can already tell you without it.

First: a reading works without it

Your BaZi chart has four pillars — year, month, day, hour. The hour pillar is the only one that needs a birth time, and it governs the finest layer: late-life detail, the children's palace, one part of the strength weighting. The day pillar (who you are) and month pillar (the season that shaped you) — the core of any serious reading — come from your birth date alone.

That's why Elun offers a proper three-pillar (三柱) reading when the time is unknown: honest about what's out of scope, exact about everything else. No guessed hour, no fabricated column.

How to actually find your birth time

1. The long-form birth certificate (US)

The short-form certificate most people have omits the time — but the long form, the "Certificate of Live Birth," usually records it to the minute. Order it from the vital-records office of the state where you were born; most states take online orders and charge $15–30. This is the single most reliable source.

2. Hospital medical records

Delivery records keep the exact time of birth. Call the records (health information) department of the hospital where you were born — you're entitled to your own records, though older ones may take a few weeks to retrieve.

3. Family memory — more useful than you'd think

BaZi doesn't need the minute. The day divides into twelve two-hour gates, so "just before dawn," "during the lunch rush," or "the late movie was on" often pins the hour pillar completely. Ask a parent, an aunt, an older sibling — someone usually remembers the scene.

4. Baby books, announcement cards, family bibles

Birth time was traditionally written in baby books and printed on birth announcements. Attics and old photo albums have settled this question more than once.

Born near midnight or on a two-hour boundary? Then precision matters doubly — the day pillar itself can shift at midnight, and Elun computes with true solar time (your birthplace's longitude), which can move the clock by over half an hour. A rough time plus your birth city is often enough for us to resolve it correctly.

What to do with whatever you have

Have a time (even approximate)? Enter it — the chart uses two-hour gates, so "around 5 am" is workable data, not a guess.

No time at all? Check "I don't know my birth time" and get the full three-pillar reading. If you later find the time, premium reports can be regenerated with the same order number — the hour pillar simply joins the story.

Either way, your chart is ready to be read — calculated with true solar time, not guessed.
